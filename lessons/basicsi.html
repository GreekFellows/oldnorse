Basics I.

Vowels, diphthongs and pronunciation.

Old Norse was originally written in runes; with the conversion to Christianity, the Latin alphabet came in. The long-trusted great work on Old Norse First Grammatical Treatise suggests the folloing vowels and their pronunciations, organized in short-long pairs. Diphthongs (see below).
au combination of ǫ + u.
ei combination of ę + i.
ey combination of ę + y.

Note the followings:

  • ę is no longer distinguished from e; e had become identical to ę;
  • ǫ́ is no longer distinguished from á; á had become identical to ǫ́;
  • æ is the long of ę.
  • œ is the long of ø.
  • ǫ is often printed as ö, as in Modern Icelandic.

There are also diphthongs. A diphthong is a vowel sound that sounds different at the beginning and the end of its pronunciation. Even though a diphthong is written as two letters, it must be pronounced continuously.
