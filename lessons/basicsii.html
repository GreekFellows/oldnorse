Basics II.

Consonants and pronunciation; I.

Here are the consonants. Consonants are free of diacritics; thus there should not be any need for a magnification feature, but it is included anyway. Click on the magnifying glass () next to each letter to see it zoomed:

b as in English "boy"
c as in English "cool".
d as in English "day". Pronounced with the tongue tip against the teeth, not against the gums.
ð as in English "this".
f Explained in next lesson.
g Explained in next lesson.
h Explained in next lesson.
j as in English "you".
k as in English "cool".
l Explained in next lesson.
m as in English "home".
n Explained in next lesson.
p Explained in next lesson.
q as in English "call". Only seen in the form of qu. Occurs in East Norse texts; treated as equivalent to k.
r Explained in next lesson.
s as in English "say".
t as in English "toy". Pronounced with the tongue tip against the teeth, not against the gums.
v Explained in next lesson.
w as in English "win". Occurs mainly in East Norse texts; treated as equivalent to v.
x as in Scots "lochs" (the "ch" is similar to German "Nacht").
z as in English "gets".
þ as in English "think".

It is strongly suggested that learners do not proceed to the next lesson until they memorize the pronunciations of consonants already explained in the table above. More complex patterns of consonant pronunciations are explained in the next lesson.
