Introduction to Old Norse.

Old Norse is a language spoken by Scandinavians sometime between 8th and 15th century (possibly earlier). There is always a strong connection between Old Norse and the image of vikings; this possibly explains why Old Norse is often referred to as "the language of vikings". However, the language is not just about man-killing and dragon-slaying; Literature, such as Norse mythology and the tales of the kings, is written in Old Norse, serving as a valuable aid for Germanic studies.

Although not all of the history of Old Norse is known, there is a commonly accepted theory. Everything begins with the Proto-Germanic dialect which was so differentiated from Proto-Indo-European (the imaginary language from which all Indo-European languages are thought to have branched; abbr. PIE) that it began its evolution independently from PIE. Proto-Germanic then branched into three distinct dialects: North, East and West Germanic. Old Norse developed from North Germanic and developed into Icelandic, Swedish, Danish, Norwegian, Gutlandish, Faroese, etc.. English and German developed from West Germanic. East Germanic developed into Gothic, which has no modern descendants. Similarities and convergences between all these Germanic languages have always prevented scholars from having a deterministic view on the early divisions of Old Norse.

A common misconception is that Old Norse is equivalent to Old Icelandic. Old Icelandic is one of the dialects of Old Norse; the other dialects include Old Norwegian, with which Old Icelandic make up the West Norse branch, and Old Swedish and Old Danish, together making up the East Norse branch. According to An Introduction to Old Norse by E. V. Gordon, Old Icelandic records are the most plentiful among all dialects; almost all Old Norse literature of any value is written in Icelandic. The study of the Old Icelandic grammar is thus taken as the basis of studying Old Norse.

Basics I.

Vowels, diphthongs and pronunciation.

Old Norse was originally written in runes; with the conversion to Christianity, the Latin alphabet came in. Note the followings:

  • ę is no longer distinguished from e; e had become identical to ę;
  • ǫ́ is no longer distinguished from á; á had become identical to ǫ́;
  • æ is the long of ę.
  • œ is the long of ø.
  • ǫ is often printed as ö, as in Modern Icelandic.

There are also diphthongs. A diphthong is a vowel sound that sounds different at the beginning and the end of its pronunciation. Even though a diphthong is written as two letters, it must be pronounced continuously.

Basics III.

Consonants and pronunciation; II.

The position of a consonant in a word determines its pronunciation; the rules must be perused and mastered if the learner wishes to speak Old Norse (strongly suggested). Here are the consonants which were not explained in the previous lesson.

  • g
  • h
  • l
  • n
  • p
  • r
  • v
f Pronounced voicelessly as in English "fat" initially (at the beginning of a word)
or when followed by a voiceless consonant.
Pronounced voicedly as in English "vet" otherwise.
Voiced f followed by an n is nasalized, as in jafn, often spelled jamn.
g Pronounced voicedly as in English "get" initially, in the combination ng or when doubled, as in gg.
Pronounced voicelessly as k when in gg or ng followed by a t or an s.

There are other rules of pronunciation.
