Introduction to Old Norse.

Old Norse is a language spoken by Scandinavians somewhen between 8th and 15th century (possibly earlier). There is always a strong connection between Old Norse and the image of vikings; this possibly explains why Old Norse is often referred to as "the language of vikings". However, the language is not just about man-killing and dragon-slaying; Literature, such as Norse mythology and the tales of the kings, is written in Old Norse, serving as valuable information for Germanic studies.

Not much of the history of Old Norse is determined yet due to similarities here and there between languages of different branches and historical stages. Everything begins with the Proto-Germanic dialect which was so differentiated from Proto-Indo-European (the imaginary language from which all Indo-European languages are thought to have branched; abbr. PIE) that it began its evolution independently from PIE. Proto-Germanic then branched into three distinct dialects: North, East and West Germanic. Old Norse developed from North Germanic and developed into Icelandic, Swedish, Danish, Norwegian, Gutlandish, Faroese, etc.. English and German developed from West Germanic. East Germanic developed into Gothic, which has no modern descendants.
