Introduction to Old Norse.

Old Norse is a language spoken by Scandinavians sometime between 8th and 15th century (possibly earlier). There is always a strong connection between Old Norse and the image of vikings; this possibly explains why Old Norse is often referred to as "the language of vikings". However, the language is not just about man-killing and dragon-slaying; Literature, such as Norse mythology and the tales of the kings, is written in Old Norse, serving as a valuable aid for Germanic studies.

Although not all of the history of Old Norse is known, there is a commonly accepted theory. Everything begins with the Proto-Germanic dialect which was so differentiated from Proto-Indo-European (the imaginary language from which all Indo-European languages are thought to have branched; abbr. PIE) that it began its evolution independently from PIE. Proto-Germanic then branched into three distinct dialects: North, East and West Germanic. Old Norse developed from North Germanic and developed into Icelandic, Swedish, Danish, Norwegian, Gutlandish, Faroese, etc.. English and German developed from West Germanic. East Germanic developed into Gothic, which has no modern descendants. Similarities and convergences between all these Germanic languages have always prevented scholars from having a deterministic view on the early divisions of Old Norse.

A common misconception is that Old Norse is equivalent to Old Icelandic. Old Icelandic is one of the dialects of Old Norse; the other dialects include Old Norwegian, with which Old Icelandic make up the West Norse branch, and Old Swedish and Old Danish, together making up the East Norse branch. According to An Introduction to Old Norse by E. V. Gordon, Old Icelandic records are the most plentiful among all dialects; almost all Old Norse literature of any value is written in Icelandic. The study of the Old Icelandic grammar is thus taken as the basis of studying Old Norse.

Introduction to This Tutorial.

This web-based tutorial is developed by Fellows "Sigurðr" Greek, an Old Norse enthusiast.

Use the pagetop-fixed navbar to navigate yourself through the tutorial.

In Lessons, there are short, organized pieces of information. The content of each lesson is kept short enough to prevent learners from feeling bored or overwhelmed.

Wordbook is a lexicon. It is a list of per-word entries, organized in parts of speech. You might not always find desired entries, but it is updated regularly anyway.

It is personally suggested by the developer that learners do not rush to the next lesson immediately after reading one; instead, learners should take time to re-read the finished lesson and strengthen their knowledge. It should also be noted that lessons differ in difficulty; for example, lessons about conjugations are much harder than those on basic pronunciation of words.

Basics III.

Consonants and pronunciation; II.

The pronunciation of a consonant is determined by many conditions, including the position of a consonant in a word, a specific consonant combination where the consonant is, and the consonant that follows or precedes it; for each pronunciation, corresponding conditions are written in bold text.

The followings must be perused and mastered if the learner wishes to speak Old Norse (strongly suggested). Here are the consonants which were not explained in the previous lesson.

f

  • Pronounced voicelessly as in English "fat" initially (at the beginning of a word)
    or when followed by a voiceless consonant.
  • Pronounced voicedly as in English "vet" otherwise.
  • Voiced f followed by an n is nasalized, as in jafn, often spelled jamn.

g

  • Pronounced voicedly as in English "get" initially, in the combination ng or when doubled, as in gg.
  • Pronounced voicelessly as k when in gg or ng followed by a t or an s.

h

  • Pronounced as in English "hue" when in hj.
  • hl, hn and hr becomes voiceless l, n and r.
  • Pronounced as in German "ach" when in hv (which later beceame kv).
  • Pronounced as in English "hello" otherwise.

l

  • Pronounced as in English "like" but with the tongue tip against the teeth, not the gum, initially, when standing next to d, l, n and r, or when following an unaccented vowel.
  • Pronouned as in English "people" otherwise except when voiceless (as in hl).

n

  • ng and nk are pronounced as in English "single" and "sink".
  • hn is pronounced as a voiceless n as in English "not".
  • Pronounced as in English "not" (voiced) otherwise.

p

  • Pronounced as in English "loft" (equivalent to voiceless f) when followed by s or t.
  • Pronounced as in English "pet" otherwise.

r

  • Strong point trill, as in Scottish.
  • Pronounced voicelessly when following a voiceless consonant (p, t, k).

v

  • Pronounced as in English "vet".
  • hv (becoming kv in some dialects) is pronounced as a voiceless v.

Double consonants followed by a vowel are actually pronounced double; kk in drekka is pronounced as in English "bookkeeper".
